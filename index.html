The Story Behind the Data

In today’s world, choosing the best place to live isn’t just about scenic views or cultural experiences—it's also about economic sustainability. Especially for those who prefer working on-site rather than remotely, selecting the right country to build a life in starts with one key question: Can I afford to live there on what I earn? To explore this, I have built an interactive Shiny for Python application that helps users compare the monthly average salary with the monthly average cost of living per person across countries.

I have gathered detailed figures on cost categories (like housing, food, and transport), average salaries, and inflation trends—all wrapped into an interactive interface where you can explore:

  • Historical trends in salary and cost of living
  • Country-to-country cost comparisons for a specific year
  • A global cost of living map
  • Inflation trajectories based on CPI data
  • Happiness scores to gauge quality of life

This application is designed to help you make informed decisions about where to live, based on real data and trends. Whether you're a digital nomad, a family looking for a new home, or just curious about global living standards, this tool is for you.

What did the data reaveal?

After comparing the numbers, some striking patterns emerged. We identified countries where the average cost of living significantly exceeds the average salary—in some cases by more than three times, like in Tajikistan. On the other end of the spectrum, there are countries where living expenses represent only a small fraction of the average income, such as Belgium or the Netherlands. " you scroll down, you'll see a smooth transition": On the left side: countries with the lowest cost-to-salary ratios — where people tend to keep more of what they earn. On the right side: countries with the highest ratios — where living takes a disproportionately large bite out of income. Once the scroll completes, only two charts remain: On the left, the country with the lowest share of living costs relative to average salary. On the right, the country with the highest share. In the center, an icon with a label: “From lowest to highest cost-to-salary ratio”

An important aspect: Inflation

How quickly prices for goods and services are rising? Below, you'll find a set of interactive charts that explore this further. We’ve included inflation trends and contributing factors (like energy prices, food, housing, and transport) for: The 10 countries with the lowest cost-of-living-to-salary ratios And the 10 countries where living expenses consume the largest share of average income You can either view stacked, overlapping charts for a dynamic comparison — or use a simple dropdown search to select the country you're interested in. 🟦 On the left, you’ll first see Belgium — where people enjoy one of the most favorable income-to-expense ratios. 🟥 On the right, you’ll see Tajikistan, where living costs often far exceed average earnings.

Take a closer look at the inflation curves, especially around 2020–2021, and you’ll likely notice a common spike. That’s no coincidence — the COVID-19 pandemic, which hit globally during those years, contributed significantly to rising inflation in many regions. This is clearly visible in the data. Explore, compare, and reflect — because understanding inflation helps you see not just how much life costs, but how quickly those costs can change.

What about security in various countries?

As seen in the comparison of countries included in this analysis, Luxembourg, Iceland, and the Netherlands have the highest safety ratings. On the other hand, Ukraine currently has the lowest safety rating, which is undoubtedly related to the ongoing conflict with Russia. (Data source: http://theglobaleconomy.com/) So, can we conclude that Luxembourg, Iceland, and the Netherlands are indeed some of the best places to live in today’s world? After all, safety is a fundamental need for all of us. Let’s take this further by comparing these findings with a somewhat less obvious measure — the happiness index. (Data source: World Happines Report )

Which country have the happiest citizens?

As shown on the map, the happiest people live in Finland (score 7.74), the Netherlands (7.52), and Iceland (7.51). All the green areas around the world represent places where people report the highest levels of happiness.
